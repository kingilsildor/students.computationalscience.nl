Student life is not always easy. Whether it is finding affordable housing, dealing with mental health problems or staying on schedule with the workload, challenges abound! On this page we will share some of the struggles of our current Computational Science students as well as things they find easy and some tips to make life as a Computational Science student easier.

To get a full view of the student experience, we did not only ask students for input, but also four teachers. The teachers we interviewed are Gábor Závodszky, Clélia de Mulatier, Vítor Vasconcelos and Mike Lees. With their knowledge on what is expected from students, seeing them succeed (or not) every year and having been a student once themselves, they have interesting insights on what life is now studying Computational Science. Of course, some things have changed since they were themselves students. For one, Clelia notes that due to technological developments, teachers are now a lot more accessible outside of the classroom, through email or Canvas. Besides the accessibility, students’ expectations have changed as well, posits Gabor. Students now expect more predictability from the teachers: they want to know more about what is asked from them in the course, for example by getting a sample exam in preparation for the real one. Finally, Vitor adds that on the content side a lot has changed as well. Now, studying has become more interdisciplinary and more aimed at applications. Studying, in his opinion, has become more impact focused, while knowing for the sake of knowing has lost some ground.

With these changes in mind, let’s dive into the experiences of students of Computational Science. We will first discuss what is easy and what is a struggle for students, both from the students’ and the teachers’ perspective. Afterwards, we will share how the students deal with stress and we will end with sharing some advice the teachers have for (prospective) Computational Science students.

What is easy?

What students think

When we categorize the students' answers to the questions what they find easy as a student, the category most mentioned is 'study related'. This category entails everything that has to do with the content of the program as well as doing the actual work on the assignments. Within this category answers differ. Some find the maths easy, while others actually think the programming is the easiest part and then there are even people who think writing the reports is easy. This very clearly reflects the broad nature of the program and especially the very diverse backgrounds of our students. Some of whom are more experienced mathematicians, some more experienced programmers and others more experienced writers. Surprisingly, the second biggest category is work-life balance. Quite a few students indicate not having any trouble balancing studying, with working, working out, having a social life and sleeping enough hours every night. An incredible feat if you ask the authors! Close to the 'work-life balance' is the category of 'socializing'. Apparently, our students love to chat!

Besides the answers that we were able to categorize, we also received a few interesting unique answers. For example, one student shared with us that they find it easy to go find a free restroom in the city centre because they can always enter the library on the Singel. Another student shares with us that they find it easy to find a good study place and that Science Park is an excellent campus.

What teachers think

For the teachers determining what students find easy was a bit difficult. However, the teachers did notice as well that due to the interdisciplinary nature of the program, students find some parts easier than others. They are impressed by the ability of most students to adapt to all the different topics of the program, create their own trajectory in their studies and be well prepared for their thesis once they get there.

What is a struggle?

What students think

There is a clear category that is by far the largest, when it comes to what students note as their (biggest) struggle: work-life balance. Students find it hard to balance it all: studying, working, exercising, sleeping enough, socializing and having hobbies. Trying to do it all, can cause a good amount of stress. Later on we will share how our students deal with this stress. The second biggest category is study-related. Here we again see the effects of the interdisciplinary master in combination with having a student body with a diverse background. Students who do not have a strong background in a certain topic, note that they struggle with that specific topic within the master.

Other interesting and noteworthy struggles are staying motivated, housing, mental health, paying the bills and socializing. One person even noted 'dating' as one of their struggles! Interestingly, a lot of the topics noted as a struggle, were also present for some students as something they find easy. When it comes to work-life balance, dealing with certain topics in the program, socializing and staying motivated, there are students who find it easy and students who struggle with it. Our students could really help each other out a lot!

What teachers think

The teachers acknowledge the difficulties in work-life balance for the students. They note that there is a lot of pressure on students to work, either for financial reasons or for boosting their CV, but that it is very difficult to combine a master's degree with working. On the content-side they also recognize that depending on their background, some students struggle more with some parts of the program than others. The interdisciplinary nature of the master is also seen as a challenge. As a student you need to be an expert in the techniques and the domain you apply it to. You need to learn the methods, but also to apply them to very different contexts.

Even though the teachers recognize the struggles of the students, they also state that the master is supposed to be challenging and that the stakes seem high but that this master also provides a safespace to make mistakes. One teacher expressed their hope that students find it easy to talk to each other and the staff whenever the challenge seems to be too high.

How do students deal with stress?

We also asked our students how they deal with stress. The two most popular answers are doing some type of work-out or planning out what needs to be done and start working even harder. Other popular answers are doing something relaxing, like gaming or reading a book, eating something (unhealthy), or socializing and finding support from other students.

There were a few answers that stood out. One student shared that they would exercise and read about real-world problems to deal with stress. There really is nothing like the current state of the world to put your personal troubles into perspective! On a related note, two students admitted to crying to release some stress. One student opts for doing something creative, while another distracts themself by going motorcycling. Other suggestions were taking a break, yoga or meditation. One student kept their answer on how they deal with stress short and sweet: 'beers'.

Hopefully, the answers give you some inspiration on how to deal with stress or, if not, that they at least are relatable. If you are looking for a bit more support, read along! We will now share the advice the teachers have for their students!

What are advices from the teachers?

Here are, in no particular order, the advices the teachers shared with us:

  • Make the program your own.
    You have a lot of options in this program and making it your own is the key to success. Think about the future, what you enjoy doing and take some time to explore!

  • Really take your breaks and holidays.
    You really need to decompress. A bit of catching up is allowed, but make sure you also take full days off from studying. One teacher confided in us that in the period they worked the hardest, they also partied the hardest. This provided the right balance they needed!

  • Work regularly.
    Find ways to really force yourself to work regularly instead of having to catch up on all the work in the last week of a course.

  • Interact with teachers.
    It can be really beneficial to talk to your teachers in class and during breaks. Most teachers appreciate this, there is no reason to be shy!

  • No need for balance.
    Studying might not be the time for balance and that is okay. Don't spend too much energy on trying to achieve that.

  • Challenge yourself.
    Studying is the perfect time to challenge yourself. Try to learn as much as possible and don't be afraid to fail. The stakes might feel high, but you are, maybe for the last time in life, in a protective bubble where you can fail and try again!

  • Enjoy!
