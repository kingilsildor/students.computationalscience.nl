Student life is not always easy. Whether it is finding affordable housing, dealing with mental health problems or staying on schedule with the workload, challenges are abound! On this page we will share some of the struggles of our current Computational Science students as well as things they find easy and some tips to make life as a Computational Science student easier.

To get a full view of the student experience, we did not only ask students for input, but also four teachers. The teachers we interviewed are Gábor Závodszky, Clélia de Mulatier, Vítor Vasconcelos and Mike Lees. With their knowledge on what is expected from students, by seeing those students succeed (or not) every year, and by having been a student once themselves, they have an unique perspective on what life is like today as a student of Computational Science. Of course, some things have changed since they were themselves students. For one, Clelia notes that due to technological developments, teachers are now a lot more accessible outside of the classroom, through email or Canvas. Besides the accessibility, students' expectations have changed as well, posits Gabor. Students now expect more predictability from the teachers: they want to know more about what is asked from them in the course, for example by getting a sample exam in preparation for the real one. Vitor adds that on the content side a lot has changed as well. Now, studying has become more interdisciplinary and more aimed at (usefull) applications. Studying, in his opinion, has become more impact focused, while knowing for the sake of knowing has lost some ground. Finally, Mike notes that there have been great developments in programming and programming languages since he was a student himself. He adds that students can take a lot more, but that there is also a lot more that is expected of them. Today, students need to be quick, but still acquire a deep understanding of the subjects.

With these changes in mind, let's dive into the experiences of students of Computational Science. We will first discuss what is easy and what is a struggle for students, both from the students' and the teachers' perspective. Afterwards, we will share how the students deal with stress and we will end with sharing some advice the teachers and we, the authors, have for (prospective) Computational Science students.

What is easy?

What students think

When we categorize the students' answers to the question what they find easy as a student, the most popular category is 'study related'. This category entails everything that has to do with the content of the program as well as doing the actual work on the assignments. Within this category answers between students differ. Some find the maths easy, while others actually think the programming is the easiest part and then there are even people who have no problems with writing the reports. This variety very clearly reflects the broad nature of the program and the very diverse backgrounds of our students, some of whom are more experienced mathematicians, some more experienced programmers and others more experienced writers. Surprisingly, the second biggest category is work-life balance. Quite a few students indicate not having any trouble balancing studying with working, exercising, having a social life and sleeping enough hours. An incredible feat if you ask the authors! Close to the 'work-life balance' is the category of 'socializing'. Apparently, a lot of our students love to chat and have a good time with each other!

Besides the answers that we were able to categorize, we also received a few interesting and unique answers. For example, one student shared with us that they find it easy to go find a free restroom in the city centre because they can always enter the library on the Singel! Another student shares with us that they find it easy to find a good study place and that Science Park is an excellent campus.

What teachers think

For the teachers determining what students find easy was a bit difficult. However, they do notice as well that due to the interdisciplinary nature of the program, some students find some parts easier than others. They are impressed by the ability of most students to adapt to all the different topics of the program, to create their own trajectory in their studies and to be well prepared for their thesis once they get there.

What is a struggle?

What students think

For this question, there is a clear category that is by far the largest when it comes to what students note as their (biggest) struggle: 'work-life balance'. Unsuprisingly, a lot of students find it hard to balance studying with working, exercising, sleeping enough, socializing and having hobbies. Trying to do it all, can cause a good amount of stress. Below we will discuss how our students deal with this stress. The second biggest category is 'study-related'. Here we again see the effects of the interdisciplinary nature of this master in combination with having a student body with diverse backgrounds. Students who do not have a strong background in a certain topic, note that they struggle with that specific topic within the master.

Other interesting and noteworthy struggles are 'staying motivated', 'housing', 'mental health', 'paying the bills' and 'socializing'. One person even noted 'dating' as one of their struggles! Interestingly, a lot of the topics noted as a struggle, were also present for some students as something they find easy. When it comes to work-life balance, dealing with certain topics in the program, socializing or staying motivated, there are students who find it easy and students who struggle with it. Our students could really help each other out a lot!

What teachers think

The teachers acknowledge the difficulties of students in maintaining a work-life balance during the master. They note that there is a lot of pressure on students to work besides their studies, either for financial reasons or for boosting their CV, but that it is also very difficult to combine a master's degree with working. On the content-side they also recognize that depending on their background, some students struggle more with some parts of the program than others. The interdisciplinary nature of the master is also seen as a challenge. As a student you need to be an expert in both the techniques and as well as the domain you apply it to. You need to learn the methods, but also learn how to apply them to very different contexts.

Even though the teachers recognize the struggles of the students, they also state that the master is supposed to be challenging and that the stakes seem high but that the program also provides a safespace to make mistakes. One teacher expressed their hope that students talk to each other and the staff whenever the challenge seems to be too high.

How do students deal with stress?

We also asked our students how they deal with stress. The two most popular answers are doing some type of work-out or planning out what needs to be done and start working even harder. Other popular answers are doing something relaxing, like gaming or reading a book, eating something (unhealthy), or socializing and finding support from other students.

There were a few answers that stood out. One student shared that they would exercise and read about real-world problems to deal with stress. There really is nothing like the current state of the world to put your personal troubles into perspective! On a related note, two students admitted to crying to release some stress. One student opts for doing something creative, while another distracts themself by going motorcycling. Other suggestions were taking a break, yoga or meditation. One student kept their answer on how they deal with stress short and sweet: 'beers'.

Hopefully, these answers give you some inspiration on how to deal with stress or, if not, that they at least are relatable and make you feel less alone. If you are looking for a bit more support, read along! We will now share what advice the teachers have for the students!

Advice from the teachers

Here are, in no particular order, the advices the teachers shared with us:

  • Make the program your own.
    You have a lot of options in this program and making it your own is the key to success. Think about the future, what you enjoy doing and take some time to explore!

  • Really take your breaks and holidays.
    You really need to decompress. A bit of catching up is allowed, but make sure you also take full days off from studying. One teacher confided in us that in the period they worked the hardest, they also partied the hardest. This provided the right balance they needed, so don't be afraid to let loose once in a while!

  • Work regularly.
    Find ways to really force yourself to work regularly instead of having to catch up on all the work in the last week of a course.

  • Interact with teachers.
    It can be really beneficial to talk to your teachers in class and during breaks. Most teachers really appreciate this, so there is no reason to be shy!

  • No need for balance.
    Studying might not be the time for balance and that is okay. Don't spend too much energy on trying to achieve that.

  • Challenge yourself.
    Studying is the perfect time to challenge yourself. Try to learn as much as possible and don't be afraid to fail. The stakes might feel high, but you are, maybe for the last time in life, in a protective bubble where you are allowed to fail and try again!

  • Enjoy!

Advice from the authors

Being Computational Science students ourselves, we, the authors, have some experience with the trials and tribulations of doing this master. To finish off this section, we would love to share some tips, again in no particular order:

  • Don’t be afraid to ask for help.
    This advice is useful and true throughout your life, but also especially during your studies. In this program, there are a lot of people you can turn to when you need help. There are teachers, TAs, tutors, the study advisor and the program coordinator, to name a few. Maybe the best and least intimidating place to start asking questions are your fellow students. As discussed before, what you are struggling with might be their expertise and if not it can be really comforting to know others are struggling with the same thing too.

  • Don’t study alone.
    Even if you are not working on the same thing, it can be really motivating to see others work (and struggle) too.

  • Aim for progress, not perfection.
    Do not waste your time trying to perfect things that are already good. In an experiment where some students had to paint one amazing painting (quality) and others had to paint 100 mediocre paintings (quantity) for their final assignment, it turned out that the quantity paintings were better than the quality ones. Sometimes, it is better to focus on finishing something and moving on, instead of trying to make something perfect. The same goes for your work schedule. Don’t spend too much time on making it perfect, it is all about making progress and getting better, not about getting it perfect!

  • Work assignments backwards.
    Sometimes, if you do not know where to start with an assignment, look at what the final product should be and work your way backwards. Ask yourself the question: ‘what do I need to figure out to be able to get the results I need?’ and then ‘what do I need to figure out to figure that previous thing out?’, etc!

  • Breaks are non negotiable!
    Everyone needs breaks. They are really important for your (mental) health and will in the long run be essential for your progress. Figure out what type of break schedule works for you: a few minutes every hour or a long one hour lunch break or no more studying after a certain time or something else!

  • Don’t procrastinate, but if you do, be kind to yourself.
    Of course, it is always better to keep to your schedule and not procrastinate, but don’t beat yourself up if you end up procrastinating anyways. Sometimes, procrastinating is not about being lazy or unmotivated, but about being overwhelmed and needing a break. There is no use in beating yourself up for not studying when you should. If you take time off, whether you feel like you deserve it or not, enjoy it! Trust you will get the work done in time and realize that even if not, the world will not end and you are allowed to make mistakes.
